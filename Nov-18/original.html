Employers Can Help Mitigate Financial Stress During Uncertainty

This year marked new terrain for all of us. We entered 2020 amid the lon- gest bull run in history and then faced a pandemic crisis not experienced for generations. In April, Edelman Financial Engines continued our existing research on employee financial well-being, understanding that many Amer- ican workers were still in whiplash — and only starting to manage financial stress during the unprecedented uncertainty.

We learned that financial well-being was already weak at the onset of Covid-19, across multiple measures, including financial stress, prosperity, and emergency readiness. More than half of workers agreed they had “a lot” of financial stress in their lives or faced severe challenges in raising $2,000 during an emergency. These senses of financial fragility are unlike- ly to dissipate anytime soon, given the unknowns of Covid-19 and the re- lated impact on the economy and market volatility.

Our research closely looked at how work performance is affected by high financial stress. As employees were grappling with the emotional stress of quarantine measures, they were often thinking about potential job loss or Covid-19 related absences. Employees told us that financial stress is detri- mental to their job performance. Areas affected according to survey re- sponses were diminished productivity, loss of focus, working when sick or tired, and sometimes even anger and tension related to their financial issues.

Retirement Income 2.0 Coming This Fall

We are very excited to share that this fall, we will be piloting significant enhancements to our current Income+ service to better address the needs of participants who are nearing or in retirement. Income+ was originally launched to provide steady life-long monthly payouts from employees’ 401(k) managed accounts. While consistency and stability of income have always been the design hallmarks of Income+, we’ve heard from plan sponsors and participants that there is a strong interest for more retirement income flexibility, which will be included in our new release.

Below is a preview of the major areas of improvements in our enhanced service:

  • Holistic income planning experience for members within seven years of retirement: All available income sources will be taken into account (including Social Security, applicable pension or other income, 401k savings, and outside assets) to help pre-retirees answer three key questions:
    • How much can I afford to spend after taxes in retirement?
    • Will my money last for my lifetime?
    • How much do I need to withdraw in order to support my desired spending?
  • Greater Income+ flexibility: Going forward, Income+ enrollees will have the ability to choose one of five strategic allocations for retirement income.
  • Income decision-making framework: We will help participants compare and evaluate various income strategies in retirement by showing how their spending amounts and shortfall risk change with different asset allocations.
  • "What if" scenarios: Participants can use sliders to evaluate how their spending amounts respond to changes in retirement age, Social Security claim age, contribution levels, and investment strategies.

Retirement Income 2.0 addresses lingering concerns that participants have about being able to combine income stability with growth potential. We believe our enhanced service will allow participants to make better and more informed decisions regarding when to retire and how to generate steady income in retirement while retaining full flexibility. Reflecting our commitment to continuous innovation, future enhancements will deliver guidance on how to draw down among multiple accounts and Roth conversion strategies

Please contact your Edelman Financial Engines Relationship Manager for more information or to request a demo of Retirement Income 2.0.

What happened
Markets rose around the world in June. The S&P 500, an index of U.S. large cap stocks, was up by 1.99 percent. Small caps were up more, rising by 3.74 percent (S&P 600). Looking abroad, we see that international stocks were up by 3.41 percent (MSCI EAFE Index). And bonds also had a good month too, with Bloomberg Barclays Aggregate Bond Index up 0.63 percent. Our simple measure of volatility in the stock market shows that June was a volatile month, with S&P 500 moving by +/- one percent on 11 of the 22 trading days of the month. This was also the end of the second quarter of the year, which followed a steep fall in the first quarter. The S&P 500 rose by 20.54% over the past three months, making it the best quarterly return since the fourth quarter of 1998.

Why it happened
Uncertainty about the economic impact of the coronavirus pandemic drove markets in June. Market participants, trying to figure out how the coronavirus will impact companies’ performance, reacted strongly to good and bad news. As a result, markets experienced repeated ups and downs.

Early in the month, the unemployment rate was reported and, instead of rising substantially as expected, actually fell slightly. In response, the S&P 500 rose by 2.6 percent on June 5th. But on the 11th the S&P 500 fell by 5.9 percent after news of spikes in coronavirus infection rates and Federal Reserve Chairman Jerome Powell predicting a slow economic recovery. Then there was positive news on the 16th as retail sales grew by 18 percent, double what was predicted, sending markets back up. Another fall in markets came on the 24th as the International Monetary Fund said that the recession was worse than it had expected. There was also news of spikes in infections, leading multiple states to pause or backtrack on their reopening plans. Towards the end of the month, markets rose once more as pending home sales and the consumer confidence score beat expectations. After all these ups and downs, the S&P 500 closed up for the month.

What this means for your participants
At Edelman Financial Engines, we build personalized portfolios that reflect your participants’ risk preferences and goals. Their portfolio is likely to have risen in June, and slightly more so if they have a higher stock allocation. But, as we continue to see market volatility, it is important that participants are comfortable with their portfolio’s risk level. Your participants can log into their account and look at the impact of changing their risk preference. Or call one of our advisors to help determine the level of risk that is right for them.
