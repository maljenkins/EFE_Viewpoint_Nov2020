As the Pandemic Surges, Americans Are Struggling Financially — and Making Decisions They May Regret

by Kelly O’Donnell, Executive Vice President and Head of Workplace

The Covid-19 pandemic is continuing to take a toll on our nation, with the more than 100,000 daily new cases confirmed today, according to John Hopkins University data. In addition to the concerns that Americans have about their health and their families, they also face very real challenges with their careers and finances.

Thousands of workers are furloughed as businesses adapt to our new reality, and many more have been laid off and are struggling to cover their living expenses, including rent, mortgage and medical bills.

As we work with clients to address these challenges, one thing remains clear: Workers across our country need help more than ever before. Our nation is stressed and swirling in a painful spiral of uncertainty surrounding the presidential and congressional elections; what will happen in the stock and bond markets; when and if another stimulus package will be available; and how long it will be before a vaccine is widely distributed. It’s not surprising that all of this uncertainty is taking its toll on the U.S. workforce.

We recently surveyed over 1,900 American retirement plan participants and found that many people are scrambling to meet their financial obligations and looking to their retirement plans to pay their bills. While this may seem like a viable solution in the short term, it comes with a heavy price. Over half (55%) of workers who have withdrawn or borrowed money from their 401(k)s ended up regretting it.

When we asked employees why they were using their retirement assets for reasons other than retirement, they blamed their lack of understanding about the implications and said they didn’t realize there were other options available to them. In fact, four in five (81%) retirement investors say they could have benefited by talking with a financial advisor before taking a loan. While we are flooded with financial advice and information from the internet, friends, family, the news and TV commercials, clearly employees are not getting the trusted advice needed when they are making life-altering financial decisions.

I believe the remedy is right in front of us. Workers deserve to have better access to high-quality financial education and objective advice. Employers are uniquely positioned to provide this. And more than 4 in 5 employees would value an employee benefit from their employer that provided access to financial advice. Help can take many forms — from increasing education on important financial concepts like budgeting or managing debt to providing financial coaches and planners at key life moments — such as onboarding at a new job or transitioning from the firm.

More good news: Many mistakes can be fixed or mitigated with proper advice. Helping employees move past their financial mistakes and to learn from them — or even better yet, avoiding them in the first place — can be an enormous benefit to help improve overall financial well-being, reduce stress, and also help improve overall wellness and productivity.

With regrets come opportunities for workers to learn and empower themselves to course correct and get back on the path to achieving their goals and realizing their dreams, whether it be buying a home, paying for a son or daughter’s college education, caring for elderly parents or investing and saving for retirement.

Share with me your ideas on how we can help employees avoid financial regrets. What do you feel needs to change?

Helping Employees Access Financial Advice Reduces Stress and Improves Financial Security

by Tia Padia, Vice President, Head of Workplace Marketing

Economic uncertainty due to Covid-19 has caused significant stress for many Americans and is inflicting additional trauma on an already fragile state of financial security. Research conducted by Edelman Financial Engines during our 2020 thought leadership series reveals that employers can help by providing advice via the workplace. In the second and third studies, we offer insights from our latest research along with prescriptive solutions outlining how lack of financial advice for employees can lead to regrettable financial decisions.

Closing the Financial Advice Gap
Our research indicates a strong desire among employees for more comprehensive financial guidance. Most U.S. employees feel that professional, conflict-free advice is out of their reach. Employers can step in and close the advice gap by helping employees get the financial advice they need — and can trust — which can have a positive impact on their ability to prepare for future disruptions and improve work productivity.

Here are our key findings:1

  • Many employees are caught in the cycle of poor financial decision-making and high financial stress. Employees with high financial stress are 1.6 times more likely to receive and act on “bad financial advice” than those with lower stress levels, while those who received and acted on “bad financial advice” are 1.4 times more likely to report “a lot of financial stress” – a vicious cycle.
  • The vast majority (74%) of employees believe financial advice offered through their employer would be valuable and decrease their financial stress.

Tapping Retirement Assets Early
Compounding financial stress is that the lack of access to financial advice can cause employees to take actions detrimental to their long-term financial security. Our research reveals Covid-19’s financial impact on employees — and actions they subsequently took, including borrowing from retirement plans. The absence of financial education and personal coaching, coupled with easy access to retirement funds, is letting employees plunge themselves into a debilitating financial crisis. There is no reason for employers to hesitate in providing additional help to their workforce — employees want their employers to help them.

Here are some key takeaways from the study:1

  • In August, half of U.S. employees said they took actions detrimental to their long-term financial security due to the pandemic; a 50% increase from April.
  • Nearly half (44%) of employees have accessed their retirement accounts early or are considering it.
  • 55% of borrowers regret their decision.

Expanded Support for Career Transition Planning

This year has been challenging for both employers and employees. In fact, 1 in 2 employers have said they’ve had or are expecting furloughs or layoffs due to Covid-19.1 Early retirement or layoffs can be highly stressful events to employees during any environment — but particularly now given the current economic uncertainty.

In response, we’ve expanded the support our National Advisor Center provides for Career Transition Planning.

This enhanced benefit is exclusive to Edelman Financial Engines employer clients as a way to help their employees navigate the financial implications resulting from corporate actions. Following a detailed, goals-based discussion and assessment process, Career Transition Planning helps employees take stock of their individual financial situation by providing the following benefits:

  • Advice: Complimentary consultation with a licensed financial professional so employees can talk through their specific situation and how the career transition impacts distribution decisions, life event planning, debt management, and much more.
  • Education: Comprehensive education specifically designed to help employees understand their options when facing early retirement or layoff decisions.

Career Transition Planning is a turnkey program that integrates one-on-one advisor access with targeted email outreach, education materials, and educational webinars — all at no additional cost. Best of all, because our licensed financial professionals act as fiduciaries in these conversations, your employees will feel supported during their transition and know the advice they are getting is always in their best interest.

Reach out to your Edelman Financial Engines consultant relations partner to discover how Career Transition Planning can help reduce employee stress during career transitions.

Communicating During Market Uncertainty

Edelman Financial Engines is committed to helping participants and plan sponsors prepare to make positive long-term decisions amid market fluctuations and economic uncertainty.

Rest assured that we’ve been educating participants on preparedness throughout the year while also prepping response plans internally to be ready for participants when they need us most. Additionally, we are watching conditions closely and will communicate regularly with plan sponsors and participants. Based on our engagement with participants in the spring, we know that they appreciate being kept informed.

Depending on the severity during any period of uncertainty, we may send plan sponsors and participants a variety of educational information and resources, including:

  • Weekly market update emails with timely perspective on topics such as economic outlook and the participant experience
  • Market uncertainty education toolkit with content and resources for employers to share with their employees
  • Market Volatility Resource Center: updated articles and resources

To learn more about how we can our mutual clients during these challenging times, please contact your Edelman Financial Engines consultant relations partner.
